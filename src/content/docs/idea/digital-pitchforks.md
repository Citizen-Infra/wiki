*Digital Pitchforks* is the manifesto behind citizen infrastructure. It's a CIBC manifesto; this version was revised by **Chris Lunch (InsightShare)**. What follows is a short distillation — the argument, not the full text.

## Give people a tool

The manifesto builds on a line from Buckminster Fuller:

> "If you want to teach people a new way of thinking, don't bother trying to teach them. Instead, give them a tool, the use of which will lead to new ways of thinking."

The claim is that tools teach. When people use platforms for genuine participation, they begin thinking like participants rather than consumers. When they coordinate through tools they control, they begin thinking like stewards rather than users. The tools "literally teach new ways of thinking — not through instruction, but through practice."

## The feudal threat

The backdrop is what the economist Yannis Varoufakis calls **techno-feudalism**: digital platforms that have replaced markets with fiefdoms, where users become digital serfs and a few barons extract wealth from the commons of human attention and data.

These tools teach too — but they teach feudal habits: submission, competition for scraps, learned helplessness in the face of algorithmic control. They are, the manifesto says, "digital pitchforks in reverse" — fragmenting solidarity instead of building it. The antidote isn't better regulation of feudal tools. It's building tools that cultivate empowerment, collective intelligence, and the capacity to act together.

## The infrastructure we need

Digital pitchforks are citizen infrastructure that cultivates empowerment and solidarity *through use*. The manifesto names six qualities they share:

- **Pedagogical by design** — every interaction teaches collective rather than individual thinking
- **Ownership-enabling** — users become stewards, not consumers
- **Solidarity-building** — designed to strengthen connection and shared purpose
- **Sovereignty-preserving** — communities keep control of their tools and data
- **Interoperable** — working together as part of a larger ecosystem
- **Antifragile** — growing stronger under stress, not more brittle

## The builders, and a guild

Behind every tool is a builder who sees the gap between what exists and what citizens need. But this work is precarious: it doesn't fit venture capital — there's "no exit strategy from empowerment" — and it falls through grant gaps, too technical for social foundations and too civic for tech foundations. So builders end up scattered and isolated.

That's the case for a **guild**: a community of practice offering shared learning, common standards, mutual support, and more sustainable funding for the people building citizen infrastructure.

## A call to action

The manifesto closes by addressing three audiences:

- **Funders** — treat citizen infrastructure as essential as physical infrastructure; fund it for the long term, not as prototype grants that vanish.
- **Builders** — you are "educators of the highest order"; join the guild; share not just your code but your wisdom.
- **Citizens** — the tools you choose teach you how to think. Demand tools that help you act as citizens, not serfs, and support the people building them.

> "The pitchfork was never just a farming tool — it was a symbol of citizen power. Today's digital pitchforks have the same potential."

And these aren't abstractions: the manifesto points to tools already being built for exactly this.
