"Technology for collective action" is the north star of citizen infrastructure. It names a meeting point between three things that usually get discussed separately: **technology**, **democracy**, and **solidarity**.

## Why those three together

Most of the technology we use is built to make us more efficient as individuals — faster shoppers, sharper performers, lonelier optimisers. Citizen infrastructure starts from a different question: *what would technology look like if its purpose were to help people act together?*

Democracy here means more than institutions on paper. It's the living capacity of people to shape the decisions that affect them. Solidarity is the willingness to act for one another, not merely alongside one another. Technology for collective action is whatever strengthens those two — tools that make organising, deciding, and sharing easier and more common.

## Not serfs of Silicon Valley

There's an edge to this. The dominant platforms have turned users into something close to digital serfs: we work the land, and the landlords take the value. The response isn't only to complain or to wait for regulation. It's to **build the tools citizens actually need** — and to own and govern them ourselves.

That's a constructive stance more than an angry one. The interesting work isn't tearing down what exists; it's making the alternative real, one tool at a time, until acting together becomes the easy and obvious thing to do.

## The citizen as builder

This is why the citizen in "citizen infrastructure" is *agentic and artisan*. You're invited not just to use better tools but to have a hand in making them — to treat the infrastructure of collective life as something you can shape, repair, and pass on.

The fullest statement of this stance is the movement's manifesto.
